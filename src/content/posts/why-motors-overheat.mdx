title: "Why motors overheat (and what your PD gains have to do with it)"
date: "2026-04-06"
description: "Aggressive PD tuning feels like better control. Until the motor hits 90°C and shuts off."
---

Motors overheat because they dissipate power as heat. The power dissipated is $P = I^2 R$.
So the question is always: what's driving current?

## The naive answer

Current tracks torque. Torque tracks error (if you're running a PD loop). So:

- Big error → big torque demand → big current → heat

If your robot falls slightly behind on a trajectory and the gains are high,
the controller commands a large corrective torque. The motor draws heavy current.
If this happens repeatedly — oscillation, sustained disturbance, bad feedforward —
thermal energy builds up faster than it dissipates.

## Why high $k_p$ is a hidden heater

With a high proportional gain, small position errors produce large torque commands.
The motor is constantly working hard to cancel tiny deviations.
From the thermal model's point of view, it's the same as running at partial load continuously.

A lower gain that tolerates a bit more error often means dramatically lower average current.

## The fix isn't always lower gains

Sometimes the issue is a bad feedforward — the controller is fighting gravity or
inertia with feedback instead of predicting it. Adding a proper gravity compensation term
or feed-forward torque can cut the steady-state current significantly without sacrificing tracking.

## What to actually monitor

- **Winding temperature** — if you have a temp sensor, plot it
- **RMS current over a trajectory** — not peak, RMS
- **Duty cycle** — how long is the motor near saturation?

Thermal margins matter more in legged robots than people expect,
because locomotion is not a short burst — it's sustained.
